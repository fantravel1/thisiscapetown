Getting Around Cape Town

How you move shapes what you see ‚Äî transport realities and responsible choices

A City That Does Not Fit in One Walk

Cape Town is not a walkable city in the way Amsterdam or Barcelona are. It is spread across a peninsula, draped over a mountain, and shaped by coastlines that stretch in every direction. The distances between neighborhoods are real distances ‚Äî measured in kilometers, shaped by geography, and deepened by a history of spatial planning that deliberately separated communities.

This means transport is not a convenience question. It is a structural one. How you move through Cape Town determines what you see, who you encounter, and whose economy you participate in. A visitor who only takes Ubers between tourist hotspots experiences a fundamentally different city than one who rides a MyCiTi bus to Sea Point or watches the minibus taxi system pulse through the city at rush hour.

None of this requires guilt. It requires awareness. Cape Town has multiple transport options that work at different levels of comfort, cost, and connection to the city's daily life. This guide covers them honestly ‚Äî what works, what does not, what is changing, and what your choices mean.

The reality: Cape Town's transport infrastructure reflects its history. Apartheid-era planning pushed communities to the periphery and built the city around cars. The result is a city where most residents depend on minibus taxis, while visitors often never leave the Uber-and-rental-car bubble. Understanding this gap is the first step toward moving through the city with awareness.

Transport Modes

How Cape Town Moves

Six ways to get around ‚Äî each with its own reach, cost, and relationship to the city.

MyCiTi Bus

Cape Town's most reliable public transport for visitors. MyCiTi covers key routes: airport to CBD, along the Atlantic coast through Green Point and Sea Point, and out to Table View and Bloubergstrand. Buses run on dedicated lanes where available, with scheduled stops and electronic displays.

How it works: Buy a myconnect card at any MyCiTi station (R35 for the card, then load credit). Tap on and tap off ‚Äî fares are distance-based. Cards are reloadable at stations and selected retailers. The airport route is the most useful for arrivals.

Uber & Bolt

Both apps work reliably across Cape Town and are used daily by residents and visitors alike. Affordable by international standards ‚Äî a trip from the CBD to Sea Point runs R40-70, airport to city center R200-350 depending on traffic. Wait times in central areas are usually under 5 minutes.

Practical notes: Rides are tracked, payment is cashless through the app, and driver details are shared. Safe and convenient, especially at night. Both apps handle airport pickups smoothly. Bolt is often slightly cheaper than Uber for the same route.

Minibus Taxis

The backbone of Cape Town's transport ‚Äî privately operated minibuses following fixed routes across the city. An estimated 60-70% of daily commuters rely on this system. Fares are very affordable (R10-25 depending on distance), paid in cash.

How to use them: Stand at a recognized stop and use a hand signal indicating your direction. Pay the driver or pass cash forward through passengers. Tell the driver your stop. This is not tourism ‚Äî it is the actual transport system that moves the majority of the city.

üöó

Car Rental

Useful for exploring the full peninsula ‚Äî Cape Point, Chapman's Peak Drive, the winelands, and the False Bay coast. Major international and local rental companies operate from the airport and city center. Driving is on the left side of the road.

What to know: Cape Town drivers are generally courteous but assertive. Parking in the CBD uses pay-and-display or informal car guards (a tip of R5-10 is customary). Petrol stations are full-service ‚Äî an attendant fills your tank. Do not leave valuables visible in the car.

üö∂

Walking

Excellent within certain neighborhoods. The Sea Point promenade is one of the best urban walks in Africa. The CBD, Gardens, Green Point, and the V&A Waterfront are all comfortable for walking. Bo-Kaap's cobbled streets reward exploration on foot.

The limits: Cape Town is not a city where you can walk between neighborhoods easily. The distances are real ‚Äî Sea Point to Muizenberg is 25km. Walking is excellent within clusters but impractical between them. Sidewalk infrastructure varies significantly by area.

üö≤

Cycling

Growing but still limited as daily transport. Dedicated cycling paths exist along parts of the Sea Point promenade, Green Point, and some CBD routes. The Cape Argus cycle tour is world-famous, and recreational cycling is popular on weekends.

Reality check: Most roads lack dedicated cycling infrastructure. The wind can be brutal, especially in summer. For visitors, cycling works well as a recreational activity in specific areas rather than as a primary transport mode. Some rental shops in Sea Point and the Waterfront offer bikes.

Practical Guide

What You Need to Know

The specific questions visitors actually face ‚Äî answered with costs, routes, and honest context.

Airport to City

Cape Town International Airport is about 20km from the city center. Three main options, each with different trade-offs.

  • MyCiTi bus (Route A01): The most affordable option. Runs from the airport to Civic Centre station in the CBD. Takes about 30-40 minutes depending on traffic. You need a myconnect card ‚Äî buy one at the airport MyCiTi kiosk before boarding. Fare is roughly R80-100
  • Uber or Bolt: The most convenient option. R200-350 depending on time of day and traffic. Direct to your accommodation. Airport pickups are well-organized at both the domestic and international terminals
  • Car rental: All major companies are in the airport terminal. If you plan to explore the peninsula or winelands, picking up a car at arrival makes sense. Pre-book for better rates

Getting Between Neighborhoods

Cape Town has walkable clusters and driveable gaps. Understanding this structure saves time and frustration.

  • Walkable cluster 1: Sea Point, Green Point, V&A Waterfront ‚Äî continuous promenade and sidewalks, the city's most pedestrian-friendly strip
  • Walkable cluster 2: CBD, Gardens, Bo-Kaap ‚Äî all within 10-15 minutes of each other on foot. Woodstock is a 20-minute walk from the CBD
  • Needs transport: Muizenberg (25km south), Constantia (15km, behind the mountain), Kalk Bay (30km along the coast), Hout Bay (20km via the mountain)
  • Best strategy: Base yourself in one cluster and use MyCiTi, Uber, or a car for cross-city trips. Do not try to walk between clusters

The Train Question

Cape Town has a Metrorail train network, but it has faced significant challenges that affect whether visitors should use it.

  • The Southern Line (Cape Town to Simon's Town via Muizenberg) is the most scenic urban rail route in South Africa. When running, it hugs the False Bay coast with extraordinary views
  • The reality: Metrorail has suffered from arson attacks, cable theft, infrastructure deterioration, and inconsistent scheduling. Service has been gradually rebuilding, but reliability varies week to week
  • Current advice: Check the latest status before planning around trains. When the Simon's Town line is running, it is genuinely worth the journey. But do not rely on it as your primary transport ‚Äî have a backup plan
  • Context: The rail system's struggles are a national infrastructure challenge, not a Cape Town quirk. It matters because functional rail would transform how millions of residents commute

Responsible Movement

How you move through Cape Town is not just logistics. It is an economic and social choice.

  • Economic reality: Taking Ubers exclusively between tourist spots keeps your spending within a narrow slice of the economy. Using MyCiTi, walking through neighborhoods, and buying from local shops distributes your presence more widely
  • The minibus taxi system employs thousands of drivers and supports entire networks of communities. Using it ‚Äî even once ‚Äî means participating in the city's actual infrastructure, not just the visitor overlay
  • Geography matters: Apartheid spatial planning pushed communities far from economic centers. The distances people commute daily in Cape Town are a direct legacy of this. Understanding that the taxi ranks you pass are economic lifelines, not curiosities, changes how you see the city
  • The question is not guilt. It is awareness. Move through Cape Town with your eyes open to the systems that surround you

Understanding the System

The Minibus Taxi System

The minibus taxi is not a Cape Town curiosity. It is a South African institution. Born from apartheid-era necessity ‚Äî when the state refused to provide adequate public transport for Black communities ‚Äî the taxi industry built itself into the largest public transport network in the country. It moves an estimated 15 million people daily across South Africa, and in Cape Town, it is the way most of the city gets to work, school, shops, and home.

The system runs on fixed routes with recognized ranks (stations) and stops. Fares are set by route and are remarkably affordable. Drivers know their routes deeply. The Toyota Quantum 15-seater is the standard vehicle ‚Äî so ubiquitous it has become a cultural icon. Taxis run from early morning until evening, with frequency determined by demand rather than a timetable. During rush hours, they move constantly.

For visitors, the minibus taxi can feel unfamiliar. There is no app. Routes are learned, not mapped. Hand signals indicate destinations. But this is not chaos ‚Äî it is a self-organized system with its own deep logic, developed over decades by communities who built what the state would not provide. It deserves understanding and respect, not exoticization.

Minibus Taxi Etiquette

If you decide to use a minibus taxi ‚Äî and you should consider it ‚Äî here is what to know.

  • Finding your taxi: Major ranks exist at the Cape Town Station, in the CBD, and in every neighborhood. Taxis also pick up along their routes at recognized stops. Ask a local or a security guard at a rank which taxi goes to your destination.
  • Signaling: Each route has a hand signal. Pointing a finger down means city center. Other gestures indicate different destinations. If unsure, simply tell the gaartjie (assistant) or driver where you are going.
  • Paying: Have cash ready ‚Äî small notes and coins. Fares are fixed by route. If you are sitting near the front, other passengers may pass their money through you to the driver. Pass it along and pass change back. This chain of trust is part of the system.
  • Your stop: Tell the driver or knock on the roof or window when you are approaching your stop. The taxi will pull over. Move with awareness ‚Äî these stops are quick.
  • Respect: This is people's daily commute, not a tourism experience. Be courteous, do not photograph people without permission, and treat the taxi like what it is ‚Äî public transport that carries the city.

How you move through a city reveals what you value. In Cape Town, the gap between the tourist transport bubble and the daily reality of most residents is a map of the country's unfinished business.

An Uber or Bolt from Cape Town International Airport to the city center (CBD, Gardens area) costs roughly R200-350 (about $11-19 USD), depending on the time of day, traffic conditions, and surge pricing. The trip takes 20-35 minutes depending on traffic.

To Sea Point, expect R250-400. To the V&A Waterfront, around R200-350. To Camps Bay, R300-450. These are estimates ‚Äî always check the app for live pricing before requesting. Rush hour (7-9am, 4-7pm) and late Friday afternoons can push prices and travel times up significantly.

The MyCiTi bus from the airport is a fraction of the cost at R80-100, if you are willing to buy a myconnect card at the airport kiosk and do not mind the slightly longer journey to the Civic Centre station.

Renting a car is worth it if you plan to explore beyond the city center. The Cape Peninsula drive (Chapman's Peak, Cape Point, Simon's Town), the winelands (Stellenbosch, Franschhoek, Paarl), and the False Bay coast are all dramatically better with your own vehicle. If you are staying only in the city bowl and Atlantic seaboard, Uber and MyCiTi will handle most of your needs.

Practical notes: Driving is on the left. South Africa uses a mix of traffic lights (called robots locally) and traffic circles. Most roads are well-maintained. Petrol stations are full-service ‚Äî an attendant fills your tank (tip R5-10). Parking in the CBD uses pay-and-display meters or informal car guards. Do not leave any valuables visible in your car, even in "safe" areas. International visitors need their home country license plus an international driving permit if the license is not in English.

Walking safety in Cape Town depends heavily on where and when. Sea Point promenade, the V&A Waterfront, Green Point, Camps Bay, Kalk Bay, and Constantia are all comfortable for daytime walking. The CBD and Gardens are fine during business hours but require more awareness after dark. Bo-Kaap is generally safe during the day.

General walking principles: stay aware of your surroundings, do not walk with expensive devices displayed, stick to populated routes, and ask locals about any areas you are unsure about. Sidewalk infrastructure varies ‚Äî some areas have excellent pedestrian paths while others have minimal or no sidewalks. At night, even in safe-feeling areas, Uber or Bolt is a reasonable choice for trips longer than a few blocks.
